Gary's money tips - I'm not saying this is gonna make you money, might even lose you money but this is what I think is good and easy to do when you're in your early 20s

Key points to know

  • Time value of money
  • Risk vs return tradeoff
  • In the long run, the market rises
  • Follow the crowd, even experts generally do worse than the crowd in the long run

Priorities for money

  • Always pay off highest interest rate debt first*
  • Then keep what you need for day to day living
  • Only then should you invest - this is because paying off debt can be thought of as an investment that returns that interest rate guaranteed
  • * - unless you have some investment device such as employer matching or the like that guarantees some return greater than the highest debt interest rate.

Day to day

    • Get borrowell and/or credit karma for free credit score tracking

Invest the rest of your money

  • Picking individual stocks is an easy way to lose money if you don't do actual research or dont know what youre doing. i dont know what im doing so i do the following:
  • Best bet is just to buy a mix of ETFs
    • You can usually open a brokerage account with your standard bank or use robinhood/wealthsimple trade, or just have a roboadvisor do this for you
      • Robinhood/wealthsimple trade offers no fees trading, whereas most brokerage accounts at banks charge you some commission per trade.
    • If you're picking your own, pick an allocation of fixed income etfs, then equity etfs. I personally aim for around 20% fixed, 80% equity. Idk who came up with this, but its pretty common to hear that you should use your age as the percent held in fixed income.
      • Just go on vanguard or blackrock (ishares) or bmo also has some and filter or search for the etfs that you want and then buy that ticker
      • Diversify the stock ETFs you pick
        • Your biggest position should just be in a large index, like an S&P 500 tracking ETF
        • note that the S&P 500 is cap weighted and has 500 companies listed on the NYSE and NASDAQ, vs the Dow which is stupidly price weighted and only has 30 or 60 companies so youre getting less diversification and an irrelevant weighting scheme since price depends on the number of shares which is arbitrarily set by companies. the NASDAQ 100 is only NASDAQ and only 100 companies. So thats why S&P 500
        • Then grab some different geographies - emerging markets, china, europe, etc...
        • Then grab some different industries - healthcare, tech, industrial, etc...
      • Rbalancing around once a year is probably good practice. Rebalancing is just selling and buying things to get back to the desired percentages for the different types of assets I mentioned (fixed income (i use bond etfs as a proxy) and stocks are the main ones)
    • If you're going with a robo, just make your portfolio the risk level option offered that matches with what you need (i think im 8 or 9 on wealthsimple and wealthfront)
      • Also there's some fees on robos, but less than a mutul fund still

  • Whatever option you pick you should know about some registered accounts
    • These are accounts that house your investments, they aren't investments themselves
    • TFSA (Canada)
      • You don't get taxed on gains
      • There's a limit to how much you can contribute (deposit) to a TFSA every year
      • But the limit has accumulated ever since you were 18
      • Basically, you can open a TFSA (blank) account, where the blank is a regular savings account, a brokerage (stock trading) account, and wealthsimple also gives you the option to make an account you have with them a tfsa. Then these accounts act the same as if it wasnt a tfsa except for the contribution restrictions and tax savings
      • you can check your limit on cra my account
    • The next three are "retirement" accounts. It's obviously best to start as soon as possible, but because they are retirement accounts, there's usually some restricitons on withdrawing money, but im not too familiar.
    • Rrsp (Canada)
      • Limit is 18% of your previous years income to a maximum of 26230
      • Limit accumulates - you can check this limit on cra my account
      • The idea behind these is that youll get taxed when you withdraw, and you wont get taxed on any income you put into these accounts. So since your retirement income is basically just what you withdraw from your retirement accounts, youll be at a lower tax bracket than now when youre still making income. So if you put money in now, youre saving taxes dependent on the difference in tax bracket
    • Roth IRA (US)
      • Limit is 5.5k each year but it doesn't accumulate
      • Can't withdraw gains until you're old (59.5 I think)
    • 401K (US, only through employer)
      • Max out whatever your employer matches (think of it as basically an insta 100% ROI)
